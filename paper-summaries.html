Simple summaries of some of Adam's Research

1. Hidden causes of sudden seizures
Link to 2013 paper in Brain

Imagine if someone you love—maybe an aging parent or a spouse—started having strange, brief movements in their face or arm dozens of times a day. It might look like twitching or brief spasms, and could be mistaken for stress, a movement disorder, or even ignored altogether. But those little movements might actually be early warning signs of a serious brain condition that leads to memory loss and dementia.

This study shows that those warning signs—called faciobrachial dystonic seizures (FBDS)—can be treated before the brain damage and memory loss start. In fact, catching them early and using the right treatment (not traditional seizure medications, but immunotherapy) could prevent permanent cognitive problems. That’s a big deal for families and caregivers, because it gives them a chance to act early and protect their loved one’s brain.

The Problem It Helps Solve

Many people who develop a condition called limbic encephalitis, often caused by the body’s immune system attacking the brain, suffer from seizures and dementia-like memory loss. These symptoms usually show up too late, when brain damage has already occurred.

This study looked at a very early warning sign—FBDS—and discovered two important things:

  • These seizures often show up before memory loss begins.
  • They don’t respond to regular seizure drugs, but they do respond to immune system treatments like steroids.

That means doctors could have a critical window of time to stop the disease before it harms the brain, but only if they recognize the seizures early and treat them correctly.

Key Findings in Plain Language

  • People with FBDS had sudden, repeated muscle contractions—often in the arm and face—up to 200 times a day.
  • These seizures often go misdiagnosed by even experienced doctors.
  • Standard seizure drugs did almost nothing to stop them.
  • Immune-based treatment (especially steroids) usually stopped the seizures completely—often within days or weeks.
  • People who got this early treatment avoided memory loss.
  • Those who didn’t get the right treatment often developed significant cognitive problems.

What This Means for You

If you or a loved one starts having unexplained, frequent muscle spasms—especially in the face or arm—it’s crucial to:

  • Push for a neurological evaluation.
  • Ask about autoimmune causes like LGI1 antibodies (a blood test can detect them).
  • Consider immunotherapy early, not just seizure medications.

Early diagnosis and the right kind of treatment might mean the difference between returning to normal life or developing serious memory problems.

2. Understanding How the Brain Develops and Why It Matters for Health, Learning, and Disease
Link to 2024 in Neuron

The human brain has a unique pattern of folds and grooves on its surface, like the wrinkles on a walnut. These folds form while we’re still in the womb and stay mostly the same throughout life. But scientists haven’t fully understood how and why these patterns develop—or what they mean for brain health and behavior.

This paper gives us a much clearer picture. Using brain scans from over 34,000 adults and hundreds of fetuses, researchers discovered a new way to classify these brain folds into two types:

  • “Linear” folds: simpler, straighter, and more consistent between people.
  • “Complex” folds: more branched, irregular, and variable.

These two types turn out to be deeply connected to how our brains develop, function, and even how they might be affected by genes or disorders.

What Problem Does It Help Solve?

Improving Brain Health Research

Doctors and scientists often look at brain structure to understand mental health disorders like autism or schizophrenia. But until now, there was no simple, universal system for describing the brain’s surface. This paper offers a new “map” that could make it easier to detect early signs of brain development issues—even before birth.

Understanding How Learning and Thinking Are Organized

The “linear” brain folds are usually linked to basic functions like movement and touch. The “complex” folds are linked to higher thinking—like memory, planning, and social skills. This finding helps explain why different parts of the brain develop at different times and how they contribute to learning and behavior.

Predicting and Diagnosing Brain Disorders

Because the linear folds are more heritable (tied to genes), they may be helpful markers for identifying risks of inherited conditions. The more complex folds, which are less genetically fixed, may be shaped more by experience or environment. This distinction can help tailor treatments or early interventions.

Why It’s Important for Americans

  • Better early diagnostics: Could lead to earlier, more accurate detection of brain development issues in infants and children—potentially improving outcomes.
  • New insight into mental health: May help explain biological differences in how disorders like autism, depression, or ADHD show up in the brain.
  • Tool for personalized medicine: The authors created an open-source toolkit that other researchers and clinicians can use to assess brain development from MRI scans—potentially speeding up progress across fields.

In short, this paper gives us a much-needed blueprint of how the brain’s wrinkles form, why they matter, and how they reflect both our genes and our environment. It’s a big step toward making brain science more useful for everyday health and well-being.

3. How Brain Wiring Connects to Life Outcomes—and Why That Matters
Link to 2022 paper in Royal Society Open Science

Imagine if doctors could one day scan your brain and get a sense of your overall well-being—not just medically, but in terms of your behavior, lifestyle, and risk for mental health issues. That’s the kind of future this research is working toward.

What’s the Problem?

Mental health issues are common and costly, but scientists still struggle to find reliable brain-based ways to detect or predict them. We know that how the brain is “wired” (its internal communication network) is linked to our emotions, behavior, and thinking—but the exact relationships are complex and not fully understood.

What Did the Study Do?

This paper replicated and extended a landmark 2015 study by looking at brain scans and lifestyle data from thousands of American children (ages 9–10) participating in the NIH-funded ABCD Study. It used advanced math to find patterns connecting brain activity with personal traits like academic performance, risk-taking, family income, and mental health symptoms.

The researchers confirmed a “positive–negative axis” of brain function:

  • Kids who scored higher on this axis tended to do better in school, had fewer behavioral issues, and came from more stable households.
  • Kids who scored lower showed more signs of difficulties like ADHD symptoms, rule-breaking, and emotional challenges.

In short, how different brain regions talk to each other during rest seems to reflect a broad spectrum of life experiences and well-being.

How Was This Done?

The team used resting-state fMRI brain scans (where kids lie still while the scanner records brain activity) and compared them with dozens of personal and family traits. They looked for patterns using a method called Canonical Correlation Analysis, which can find hidden connections between two big sets of data—in this case, brain wiring and behavior/lifestyle.

Why Should the Average American Care?

  • Early Detection: If patterns like this hold up over time, doctors and educators might one day be able to spot kids who are at risk of behavioral or mental health challenges before those issues surface.
  • Personalized Support: The results could help develop customized education or therapy plans based on how each child’s brain is developing.
  • Mental Health Innovation: This kind of brain–behavior mapping could one day complement (not replace) traditional mental health assessments, offering a more objective and science-based tool for intervention.

Big Picture Impact

This study supports the idea that there’s a deep link between how our brains function and the lives we lead—even in childhood. By confirming earlier findings in a new, younger, and more diverse population, it strengthens the case for using brain imaging as part of future public health and education strategies.
